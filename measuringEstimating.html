Measuring & Estimating

In physics, as well as other sciences, measuring isn't as straight forward as it is in normal life. Every measurement we record is an estimation and the true value is subject to uncertainty therefor when we measure we can only know the true value within a certain range. Also, we will frequently use estimations rounded to a number of significant figures often expressed in scientific notation. We will be covering all of this in lessons below.

Uncertainty

When taking a measurement we use a measurement instrument to make an estimation of the true value. The true value will be in the range of the estimation +/- uncertainty. To find the uncertainty inherent in the measurement instrument we will take the smallest increment measurable and divide it by two.
Example: The smallest increment a meter stick can measure is 1 millimeter. Therefor the uncertainty of a measurement using a meter stick is 1mm/2. If you trace a 5 cm line using the meter stick we can only the certain the length of the line will be within the range 5cm +/- .5mm.
However, the uncertainty for digital measurement instruments is just simply the smallest measurable increment.

Significant Figures

Significant figures are the digits in the number that show the precision of the value. .0220 has three significant figures, 220, these are significant because they show how precise the value is. Let's say that it's .0220km, that would be 220m, in this case that last zero shows us the precision of the value. the measurement was taken in meters.
Example: With a meter stick we measure a square to find the area of the square and we measure the square to be 0.0790m by 0.0590m.
7.90m ⋅ 5.90m = 46.61m2
Both of are multiplicands have 3 significant figures but it appears the product has 4 significant figures and in this case it does. 7.90 and 5.90 have the trailing zeros it shows their precision is to the hundredths and so even though the multiplicands have three significant figures the product has 4 because it is only as precise as the initial values. If the initial values were 7.9 and 5.9 the product would have three significant figures as 46.6.

Scientific Notation

Scientific notation is very useful for condensing extreme large or extremely tiny numbers. It uses the inherent quality of the base 10 positional number system to easily expand for reduce numbers by powers of ten. The number will be in the form x ⋅ 10n The rule of thumb is how many places you move the decimal point to the left is the number by which you must increase the power, while how many decimal places you move to the right is how many decimal points you need to decrease the power.
Examples:
59000 = 59 ⋅ 103
.0002345 = 2.345 ⋅ 10-4
